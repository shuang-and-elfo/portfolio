Designing Performant Ad Formats that Feel Native on Snapchat

Role

Design Lead

Company

Snap Inc.

Timeline

2021–2024

Scope

Full-funnel ad formats

Over three years, I led the design of ad formats across Snapchat's surfaces, shaping how ads across different verticals look and behave. I helped evolve Snapchat's advertising experience from a patchwork of legacy patterns into a more mature, scalable system, while also driving improvements in ad performance and revenue.

The work focused on making ads feel native to the product, grounding design decisions in real user behavior, and improving ad performance by earning consideration rather than forcing clicks.

Below are a few selected highlights and key learnings from this work.

Winning the First Few Seconds

The first and most important step in the ad funnel is not the click. It is getting people to stay long enough to give the ad a real chance.

On Snapchat, people move fast. Tapping to skip is instinctive, not dismissive. Through behavioral analysis and research, we learned that consideration only begins after users spend a few seconds with the content. If an ad fails to earn that moment, everything downstream—clicks, installs, conversions—is compromised.

I focused on making the opening moments of an ad easier to engage with. The goal was to quickly communicate value and relevance so users were more likely to pause instead of immediately skipping.

Turning Attention into Consideration

Users who remain after the first few seconds are no longer passive viewers. They are showing early intent, and the experience should respond accordingly.

Research and behavioral data showed that consideration builds gradually. Higher-quality engagement begins several seconds into the experience, not immediately on load. Showing all interactive elements upfront often overwhelms users or distracts from the content itself.

I led multiple projects focused on supporting consideration for these deeper viewers.

Progressive Disclosure

Instead of presenting all actions at once, the ad experience evolves over time. The ad card appears after a short delay, then expands and becomes more prominent later, aligning with moments when users are more likely to engage intentionally.

We tested several variants of the delay (500ms, 750ms, 1000ms) and expansion (2.5s, 3s, 4s) and launched the most performant one (1s, 3s). Accidental clicks were reduced by 47%, while high-quality clicks increased by 33%.

Redesigned Dynamic Product Ads

Eye-tracking studies showed that visual attention concentrated near the top and center of the screen, while most of our legacy CTAs lived lower down. I redesigned these formats with larger, more prominent product tiles placed in attention-dense areas so interaction aligned with where people naturally look.

Impact: This became the biggest product ad format evolution in the last 5 years. The launch drove +89.2% conversion rate and +39% dynamic product ad revenue.

We Do Not Want Just Clicks. We Want the Good Clicks.

Many early interactions with an ad can be accidental, driven by navigation rather than interest. Optimizing for raw click volume creates noisy signals and degrades both user experience and advertiser performance.

To address this, I focused on designing guardrails that protect intent.

I defined clearer navigation and activation zones so skipping content remained easy, while intentional engagement required deliberate action.

At the system level, this work shifted success metrics away from raw CTR toward higher-quality engagement and more reliable downstream signals.

The Most Effective Ads Do Not Feel Like Ads

Across all stages of the funnel, the strongest results consistently came from ads that felt native to the product.

A significant part of my work focused on cleaning up and unifying ad UI so formats felt consistent with Snapchat's organic language. This included reducing visual clutter, borrowing from familiar elements like captions and stickers, and aligning interaction patterns across surfaces so ads no longer felt layered on top of the experience.

Sponsored Snaps

This philosophy culminated in Sponsored Snaps, Snapchat's first ad product in Chat and the most sensitive surface in the app.

As the lead designer, I took Sponsored Snaps from early concept through executive pitch and approval, defining how ads could exist in a highly personal, high-frequency space without breaking social norms. The guiding principle was simple: it should feel like your favorite brand sent you a Snap, not like you were shown an ad.

Imagine getting a Snap from Starbucks. You open it, reply with a quick emoji, and suddenly you're craving that pumpkin spice latte. That's the experience we designed for—Sponsored Snaps blend seamlessly into the chat feed, reuse familiar affordances, and respect expectations users already have for messaging.

We've since expanded Sponsored Snaps beyond brand takeovers. Creators can now send Snaps directly to their followers on special occasions like birthdays, creating direct, personal engagement with fans.

Sponsored Slots on Maps

The same principle—ads that don't feel like ads—drove a newer format idea. We used to show ads mainly for physical stores, which captured only a small slice of the real economy. I proposed adding lightweight, contextual ad slots in places where people already expect to see promotion in the real world: for example, billboards along highways in Maps. That concept became Sponsored Slots on Maps. The pitch was well received by leadership and is being prioritized.

Takeaway

Working in this space made it clear how sensitive monetization surfaces really are. Small decisions about timing, placement, and interaction can determine whether an ad earns attention or gets dismissed as noise.

The strongest results came not from pushing harder on CTAs, but from designing formats that respect user intent and give people time to decide. That principle continues to guide how I approach monetization design.
